Case Study — General Motors

GM Mobile Companion App

Defining the connected vehicle experience across iOS and Android. Designing features that gave the app real utility, justified subscription value, and kept GM competitive as the industry moved into the EV era.

Senior Product Designer
iOS & Android
2020 – 2024

The Challenge

An app that needed to earn its place

Connected vehicle apps had become table stakes across the industry. Every major OEM had one, which meant having an app wasn't enough. It had to be genuinely useful. For GM's companion app, that meant designing features that gave customers a real reason to open it, and a real reason to keep paying for the subscription.

The competitive landscape was crowded and customers were increasingly savvy. Trust in large manufacturers wasn't a given. The app had to earn that trust through reliability, clarity, and features that actually delivered on the promise of a connected vehicle.

When GM moved into the EV segment, that challenge intensified. Electric vehicles introduced a new category of utility: charging status, range visibility, remote pre-conditioning. The app became the primary interface between the driver and the vehicle in ways that simply didn't exist for ICE models.

I was a designer assigned specifically to the companion app, responsible for a core set of features that formed the backbone of the vehicle management experience.

Design Challenges

What made this work hard

01

Subscription value, feature by feature

Every feature I owned had to justify its place in a paid subscription. That meant designing for immediate comprehension and recurring utility, not just first-use impressions. If a customer opened vehicle status once a week, it needed to be worth opening every time.

02

Earning trust through transparency

App store feedback consistently flagged customer skepticism, not with the design, but with the brand. Billing confusion, connectivity gaps, and the general wariness people have toward big manufacturers. My job was to design around those pain points with clarity, not paper over them. Features like the alert system and diagnostics had to be honest first, polished second.

03

EV raised the stakes for real-time data

Internal combustion vehicles and electric vehicles have fundamentally different relationships with the app. For EV owners, the app is a live instrument: charge levels, range estimates, pre-conditioning schedules. The vehicle status and health widget system I owned had to grow into that without losing coherence for ICE customers still on the platform.

04

Competing with a moving target

Other OEMs were iterating fast. Tesla, Ford, and others were shipping new app capabilities tied to new vehicle hardware. Keeping GM competitive meant consistently raising the quality bar on the features I owned while anticipating what new vehicle model years would unlock in the app experience.

Constraints

Working within the boundaries

Platform parity Features had to work consistently across iOS and Android, with different interaction patterns, OS-level behaviors, and release cycles to navigate.
Vehicle dependency Many features were directly tied to vehicle hardware capabilities, which varied by model year, trim level, and whether the customer had an ICE or EV. Design had to account for graceful degradation when features weren't available.
Subscription model Connected services were subscription-based, meaning design decisions intersected with billing, upsell flows, and the ongoing need to communicate ongoing value to active subscribers.
Security & compliance App sign-in, account security, and notification preferences were subject to security standards, legal review, and privacy regulations across multiple markets.
Legacy architecture The app had years of accumulated features, user flows, and technical constraints. New design work had to integrate cleanly rather than disrupt what existed, particularly for long-tenured subscribers.
OEM brand standards GM's design system and brand standards applied across every touchpoint: visual, motion, and interaction, with General Counsel sign-off required on any significant interface changes.

Process

How the work got done

Phase 01

Discovery & Competitive Audit

Each new feature or improvement started with understanding what already existed, in the app and in the competitive landscape. Auditing peer OEM apps, reviewing app store feedback, and talking with product and engineering to map where the gaps were.

Phase 02

User Flow & Feature Mapping

Flow before visuals. For features like diagnostics, the alert system, and WiFi connectivity, the primary design challenge was structural, determining the right decision points, states, and paths before any comps were made.

Phase 03

Visual Design & Iteration

High-fidelity comps built in Figma, working within GM's design system. Iterating with product and stakeholders across multiple rounds, documenting rationale for every significant decision, especially where the design pushed against existing patterns.

Phase 04

Stakeholder Review & Validation

Presenting to product, engineering, and legal. User testing where time allowed. Tracking feedback against design intent and documenting trade-offs when business constraints required compromise.

Phase 05

Handoff & Design QA

Detailed developer handoff via Figma, including specs, motion notes, and edge case documentation. Followed by QA pass after implementation to catch drift between spec and build, particularly on the details that matter at small screen sizes.

Feature Ownership

The experiences I designed

Within the broader companion app team, I owned a specific set of features that spanned vehicle management, account infrastructure, and connectivity. Each had its own design challenges and stakeholder dependencies.

Vehicle Health

Vehicle Status & Diagnostics

Real-time visibility into vehicle health, system status, and diagnostic alerts. Designed to be scannable at a glance, with appropriate depth for customers who wanted more detail, from tire pressure to engine indicators.

Vehicle Health

Health Widgets & Details Screen

Persistent health widgets and the full details view that surfaced when customers needed to dig deeper. Designed to work coherently for both ICE and EV vehicles, with EV-specific states for charge level, range, and pre-conditioning.

Proactive Communication

Alert System

The notification layer that told customers when something needed their attention, from low tire pressure to service reminders to charging interruptions. Clear, actionable, and calibrated to avoid alert fatigue.

Communication

Message Inbox

In-app messaging from GM: service notifications, connected service updates, and account communications. Designed as a trusted, low-noise channel that customers would actually check.

Account & Security

App Sign-In & Security

Authentication flows and security settings, including multi-factor options, biometric sign-in, and session management. Balancing friction against the sensitivity of vehicle access.

Account & Connectivity

Notification Preferences & WiFi Connectivity

Customer control over how and when the app communicates with them, plus the WiFi hotspot management experience for connected vehicles. A practical feature that became increasingly important as customers expected always-on connectivity.

Outcomes

What the work produced

GM's connected vehicle apps were among the most downloaded in the automotive category. The design consistently held up. App store feedback pointed to connectivity and billing issues as friction points, not the interface. JD Power repeatedly recognized GM as a leader in the connected vehicle space, validating the overall experience year over year.

Reflection

What I took away from this work

The GM Companion App was the longest sustained design engagement of my career and the work where I developed the most depth. Owning features across four-plus years meant seeing what happened after launch. Understanding what held up, what needed revision, and how customer needs shifted as the platform grew and the vehicle lineup expanded.

The EV transition was the most interesting inflection point. It wasn't just new features on the same foundation. Electric vehicles changed the relationship between driver and app in fundamental ways, and the design system I was working within had to stretch to accommodate that without breaking coherence for customers who had been using the app for years on ICE vehicles.

One consistent lesson from this work: utility beats novelty. Features that reliably answered a customer's real question (what's my charge level, does my car need service, did that alert mean something serious) earned trust over time. Features that were clever but unclear didn't. That shaped how I approached every design decision on this project and carried into everything I've done since.

Working within a larger design org also meant getting good at advocating for design decisions across a complex stakeholder environment: product, engineering, legal, and analytics all had skin in the game. Making the case for quality clearly and with supporting rationale became as important as the quality itself.

"Utility beats novelty. Features that reliably answered a real question earned trust over time."
